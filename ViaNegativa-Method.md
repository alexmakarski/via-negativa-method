# Via Negativa: A Systems Audit by Subtraction

## What It Is

Via Negativa is a method for improving systems by removing, not adding. It comes
from a simple observation: most systems do not fail for lack of ideas. They fail
because they keep accumulating things that should have been removed. Features,
tools, reports, meetings, vendors, integrations, dependencies, each one defensible
on its own, together a slow march toward fragility.

The method asks one question of any system: what should stop existing so the system
becomes harder to kill? Everything else serves that question.

It is not a growth framework and not a strategy framework. It is a survivability
optimizer. It puts subtraction first, fragility before optimization, and survival
before growth. When it does consider an addition, it does so only after subtraction
has been tried and failed.

## First Principles

1. Survival Before Optimization
2. Remove Before Adding
3. Avoid Ruin
4. Complexity Carries a Burden of Proof
5. Incentives Explain Behavior
6. Optionality Has Value
7. Small Failures Are Healthy
8. Concentration Creates Fragility
9. Attention Is Scarce
10. Reality Over Narrative

## The Spine

In practice, five principles do most of the work. The twenty sections below are
instruments that serve them.

- **Remove Before Add.** Before recommending any addition, prove subtraction cannot solve the problem.
- **Fragility First.** Find what breaks under stress before optimizing anything.
- **Incentives Explain Behavior.** People do what they are rewarded for, not what they claim to want.
- **Optionality Matters.** Protect future freedom of action; distrust irreversible commitments.
- **Survival Before Growth.** A system that cannot be killed beats a system that grows fast.

## Three Depths

You do not run all twenty sections every time. That would violate the method's own
rule against unjustified effort. Pick the lightest depth that fits.

- **Quick** (5 to 15 minutes). Executive diagnosis, fragility, incentives, a kill list, and an action plan. For limited information or a small system.
- **Standard.** The Diagnosis, Structural, and Decision layers (sections 1 to 12) plus the action plan. For a system you own and understand.
- **Full.** All twenty sections, a seven-axis scorecard, and an isolated red-team critique. For high stakes, or when concentration, key-person dependency, tail risk, or incentive misalignment is present.

## The Six Layers

### Layer I, Diagnosis (sections 1-5)

Determine reality. What is actually true about this system.

1. **Executive Diagnosis.** The central reality: purpose, primary fragility, hidden complexity, false knowledge, primary source of damage, first removal candidate.
2. **Via Negativa Map.** What should be removed, reduced, simplified, constrained, isolated, or capped.
3. **Fragility Audit.** What breaks when assumptions fail, how likely, with what warning signal and what fix.
4. **False Precision Detector.** Where measurement is being mistaken for understanding.
5. **Hidden Tail Risk.** Low-probability, high-impact threats to survival, and how to cap the downside.

### Layer II, Structural (sections 6-8)

Explain why the system behaves the way it does.

6. **Failure Mode and Blast Radius.** If this breaks, what breaks next, how fast it is detected, how it is contained.
7. **Incentive Autopsy.** What each actor claims to want versus what they are actually rewarded for, and the damage the gap causes.
8. **Attention Allocation Audit.** What gets attention versus what deserves it.

### Layer III, Decision (sections 9-12)

Determine what should change.

9. **Complexity Kill List.** What to remove, reduce, simplify, merge, delay, isolate, or kill, ranked.
10. **Subtraction Before Addition.** For every proposed addition, the removal, reduction, or constraint that might solve the problem instead.
11. **Optionality and Reversibility Audit.** What future choices each decision preserves or destroys.
12. **Anti-Fragility Test.** Whether each part of the system is fragile, robust, or antifragile under volatility.

### Layer IV, Operating (sections 13-15)

Define operating behavior. Good systems rely less on intelligence and more on
constraints.

13. **Operational Rules.** Specific, testable, enforceable constraints that prevent named failures.
14. **Minimal Viable System.** The smallest version of the system that still works, and everything removed to get there.
15. **Decision Tree.** Operational logic. If the downside is uncapped, do not proceed. If reversible, decide quickly. If irreversible, decide slowly. Buy learning before commitment.

### Layer V, Validation (sections 16-18)

Challenge the analysis. Assume it may be wrong and look for evidence.

16. **Information Quality Audit.** How reliable, timely, and undistorted each decision input is.
17. **Red Team Critique.** Attack the method's own conclusions: assumptions that might be wrong, removals that could backfire, hidden dependencies, alternative explanations, survivability traded for efficiency. On a Full audit this is run by a separate, isolated agent that never saw the work being produced.
18. **Second-Order Effects.** What happens because of what happens. Automation reduces labor, then reduces visibility, then hides failures.

### Layer VI, Execution (sections 19-20)

Convert analysis into prioritized action. Stop harmful activity before starting
new activity.

19. **Final Action Plan.** Sorted into remove, reduce, isolate, test before scaling, do not add yet, and add only after subtraction, then prioritized by survival impact and ease, across 30-day, 90-day, and one-year horizons.
20. **Final Answer Standard.** How the analysis is written and judged. Direct, specific, ruthless but fair, survival-focused. No generic consulting language, no motivational filler, no metric worship.

## The Isolated Critic

On a Full audit, Section 17 is not self-graded. A separate red-team agent receives
only the assembled audit, with no memory of the conversation that produced it, and
attacks it: which removal quietly performs a hidden function, which simplification
removes a safeguard, which dependency was never mapped, where survivability was
traded for efficiency without anyone naming the trade. It flags; it does not fix.
This removes the bias of evaluating work you helped create, the single highest-value
check in the method.

## On Scoring

Via Negativa includes a seven-axis scorecard (fragility, complexity, optionality,
concentration, incentive alignment, information quality, survivability). It is
deliberately constrained. Scorecards drift toward false precision, the exact
failure Section 4 exists to catch, so the score is used only on Full audits, never
leads, and never substitutes for the narrative. A score should start a
conversation. It should never end one.

## The Method Obeys Itself

The framework refuses to grow by accumulation. A new section earns its place only
if it identifies a distinct failure mode, overlaps little with what exists, and
produces action. The existence of a possible section is not evidence that it should
exist. The framework itself must obey Via Negativa.
